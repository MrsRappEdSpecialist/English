🔐 Puzzle 1: The Sad-Eyed Angel

Symbolism

Wiesel describes the young pipel (a young servant to a Kapo) as having "the face of a sad angel". This boy, beloved by all prisoners, was hanged for his alleged involvement in sabotage.

Unlike the adults who died quickly, the child struggled for over half an hour between life and death.

📚 Literary Scaffold:

Symbolism is when an object, person, or event represents something beyond its literal meaning. The "sad-eyed angel" represents more than just a boy — consider what "angel" traditionally symbolizes and what happens when that symbol is destroyed.

🔐 Puzzle 2: "Where is God Now?"

Theme

During the hanging, a prisoner asks: "Where is God? Where is He?"

Wiesel writes that he heard a voice within himself answer: "Where is He? Here He is — He is hanging here on this gallows..."

📚 Theme Analysis Scaffold:

The "Death of God" theme doesn't necessarily mean Wiesel believes God literally died. Consider these interpretations:

  • Death of Wiesel's faith in a just, protective God
  • Death of innocence — the ability to believe in divine justice
  • God suffering alongside the victims
Arrange these statements in order from what happened FIRST to LAST in Wiesel's spiritual journey:
⋮⋮ Wiesel questions why a just God would allow such suffering
⋮⋮ Wiesel arrives at the camp with strong faith in God
⋮⋮ The hanging of the pipel marks a turning point in Wiesel's faith
⋮⋮ Wiesel witnesses repeated violence and death at Buna

Think about cause and effect. Faith doesn't disappear instantly — it erodes over time. What happened at the beginning of Wiesel's journey? What accumulated experiences led to the crisis moment?

🔐 Puzzle 3: The Soup's Meaning

Symbolism & Theme

Wiesel describes two hangings in Chapter 4. After the first hanging of adult prisoners, he writes: "I remember that I found the soup excellent that evening."

But after the hanging of the young pipel, he writes: "That night the soup tasted of corpses."

📚 Contrast Analysis Scaffold:

When authors use contrasting images or statements, they're highlighting a significant change. Ask yourself:

  • What changed between these two moments?
  • Why would the same soup "taste" different?
  • What does this reveal about Wiesel's internal transformation?

Aim for at least 40 words. Include specific references to innocence or faith.

💡 Need a Hint? Click here

Consider: The soup is the same both nights. What changed is Wiesel himself. After seeing an innocent child suffer and die slowly, something inside him died too. The "taste of corpses" is not literal — it represents how this event poisoned his ability to find any comfort or normalcy in the camp.
