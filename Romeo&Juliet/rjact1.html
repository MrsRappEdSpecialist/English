Scene 1: The Brawl & The Broken Heart

Focus: Vocabulary & Author's Craft (Oxymorons)

Romeo's Heavy Heart

Romeo was not at the morning brawl. He is wandering the woods, depressed because the girl he loves (Rosaline) does not love him back. Notice how he combines opposite words to show his confusion.

ROMEO:
Here's much to do with hate, but more with love.
Why then, O brawling love, O loving hate,
O anything of nothing first created!
O heavy lightness, serious vanity,
Misshapen chaos of well-seeming forms!
Feather of lead, bright smoke, cold fire, sick health,
Still-waking sleep, that is not what it is!
This love feel I, that feel no love in this.

Scene 2: The Suitor

Focus: Reading Comprehension & Character Motives

A Father's Protective Will

Count Paris asks Lord Capulet for permission to marry Juliet. Pay close attention to Capulet's reasoning.

CAPULET:
But saying o'er what I have said before:
My child is yet a stranger in the world;
She hath not seen the change of fourteen years,
Let two more summers wither in their pride,
Ere we may think her ripe to be a bride.

PARIS:
Younger than she are happy mothers made.

CAPULET:
And too soon marred are those so early made.
The earth hath swallow'd all my hopes but she,
She is the hopeful lady of my earth:
But woo her, gentle Paris, get her heart,
My will to her consent is but a part...

Scene 3: The Book Metaphor

Focus: Theme & Figurative Language (Extended Metaphors)

Lady Capulet's Pitch

Lady Capulet tries to convince Juliet to marry Paris. She uses an extended metaphor, comparing Paris to a beautiful book.

LADY CAPULET:
Read o'er the volume of young Paris' face,
And find delight writ there with beauty's pen;
Examine every married lineament,
And see how one another lends content
And what obscured in this fair volume lies
Find written in the margent (margins) of his eyes.
This precious book of love, this unbound lover,
To beautify him, only lacks a cover.

Scene 4: Queen Mab

Focus: Tone Analysis

Mercutio's Descent

Romeo says he had a bad dream. Mercutio mocks him by telling a story about Queen Mab, the fairies' midwife who brings dreams. Notice how the tone shifts from playful fantasy to violent nightmare.

MERCUTIO:
O, then, I see Queen Mab hath been with you.
She is the fairies' midwife, and she comes
In shape no bigger than an agate-stone...
Her chariot is an empty hazelnut...
And in this state she gallops night by night
Through lovers' brains, and then they dream of love...
[The speech continues and grows darker...]
Sometime she driveth o'er a soldier's neck,
And then dreams he of cutting foreign throats,
Of breaches, ambuscadoes, Spanish blades...
And being thus frighted swears a prayer or two
And sleeps again. This is that very Mab...

Scene 5: The Shrine

Focus: Vocabulary in Context & Religious Imagery

The First Conversation

When Romeo and Juliet first meet, their dialogue perfectly forms a 14-line Sonnet. They use deep religious vocabulary to flirt.

ROMEO:
If I profane with my unworthiest hand
This holy shrine, the gentle fine is this:
My lips, two blushing pilgrims, ready stand
To smooth that rough touch with a tender kiss.

JULIET:
Good pilgrim, you do wrong your hand too much,
Which mannerly devotion shows in this;
For saints have hands that pilgrims' hands do touch,
And palm to palm is holy palmers' kiss.
